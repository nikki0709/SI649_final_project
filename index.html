America's Best Friend: The Changing Face of Dog Breed Popularity in the U.S.

How a decade of shifting preferences reveals changing lifestyles, regional cultures, and the rise of the French Bulldog

By Nikki Yuan and Ray Wu | SI649 Narrative Visualization Project

In 2015, the Labrador Retriever sat comfortably at the top of America's most popular dog breeds, a position it had held for decades. Fast forward to 2024, and a new champion has emerged: the French Bulldog. This shift represents more than just changing tastes—it reflects deeper transformations in American life, from urban living patterns to social media influence, from housing choices to regional preferences.

Over the past decade, dog breed popularity has shifted dramatically across the United States. Some breeds have climbed steadily in the rankings, while others have fallen. Regional differences reveal fascinating patterns about how Americans live, work, and connect with their pets. This article explores these trends through data, revealing what our choice of canine companions tells us about ourselves.

The Pet Landscape: Dogs Dominate American Households

Before diving into breed-specific trends, it's important to understand the broader pet ownership landscape in America. Dogs aren't just popular—they're the most common pet in American households by a significant margin.

According to the American Veterinary Medical Association, nearly half of all U.S. households include a dog. This represents millions of families making daily decisions about exercise, housing, and lifestyle that accommodate their four-legged family members.

The visualization above tells a dual story. Each bar's height shows the raw number—millions of American households that welcome pets into their homes. But look closer at the colors: the deeper the blue, the higher the percentage of all U.S. households. Dogs don't just lead in absolute numbers; they dominate in prevalence too, with nearly half of all American families choosing a canine companion. This dual encoding reveals both the scale and the reach of pet ownership—59.8 million households with dogs means millions of daily decisions about walks, vet visits, and lifestyle accommodations.

The dominance of dogs in American pet ownership sets the stage for understanding why breed popularity matters. With 45.5% of households owning dogs, even small shifts in breed preferences represent significant numbers of animals and reflect broader cultural changes. As we'll see, these choices aren't random—they mirror how we live, where we live, and what we value.

Regional Devotion: Where Americans Go the Extra Mile for Their Dogs

Not all dog owners are created equal—at least, not in their level of commitment. Some states show remarkable dedication to their canine companions, with dog owners making significant life changes to accommodate their pets. This "devotion score" measures behaviors like ending relationships over dog-related conflicts or moving to homes with yards specifically for a dog's benefit.

Colorado leads the nation in dog owner devotion, with nearly one in five dog owners reporting they moved from an apartment to a house so their dog would have a yard. Virginia and Georgia follow closely, showing that dog devotion isn't limited to any single region but appears concentrated in certain states.

Explore the map above—hover over any state to see its devotion metrics, or click two states to compare them side-by-side. The comparison panel reveals fascinating differences: Colorado's top-ranking devotion score of 100 contrasts sharply with Pennsylvania's score of 0, while states like Nevada show that 20% of dog owners moved specifically to give their dogs a yard. These regional patterns help explain breed preferences. States with higher devotion scores might favor breeds that require more space or exercise, while urban states might lean toward smaller, apartment-friendly breeds. The data reveals a complex relationship between lifestyle, geography, and breed choice—one that becomes even more apparent when we examine which breeds Americans are actually choosing.

A Decade of Change: The Rise and Fall of America's Favorite Breeds

The most dramatic story in American dog breed popularity is the ascent of the French Bulldog. In 2015, it ranked sixth. By 2024, it claimed the top spot, unseating the long-reigning Labrador Retriever. This shift didn't happen overnight—it represents a steady climb that accelerated in recent years.

The French Bulldog's rise coincides with several cultural trends: increased urban living, the influence of social media (where "Frenchie" accounts have millions of followers), and a preference for smaller, lower-energy dogs that fit modern lifestyles. Meanwhile, traditional favorites like the Labrador Retriever and Golden Retriever have remained consistently popular, even as they've been overtaken at the top.

The visualization above captures a decade of change in a single view. Each line traces a breed's journey through the rankings—watch how the French Bulldog's red line climbs from rank 6 in 2015 to rank 1 by 2021, where it remains today. Click any breed name in the legend to highlight its path and dim the others, revealing individual stories within the larger narrative. The Labrador Retriever's gray line shows a different story: steady dominance at rank 1 for six years, then a gentle slide to rank 2 as the French Bulldog claimed the throne.

These trajectories reveal more than just rankings—they tell stories of cultural shifts. The French Bulldog's meteoric rise coincides with the era of Instagram pets and urban apartment living. Meanwhile, traditional favorites like the Labrador Retriever and Golden Retriever maintain their positions in the top tier, suggesting that family-friendly breeds remain valued even as new favorites emerge. The German Shepherd Dog's consistent presence in the top five throughout the decade reflects its versatility and enduring appeal. Some breeds rise quickly, others fall gradually, and a few maintain steady positions—each line a testament to changing American lifestyles and preferences.

What These Trends Tell Us

The changing landscape of dog breed popularity in America reflects broader societal shifts. The rise of smaller, apartment-friendly breeds like the French Bulldog mirrors increasing urbanization and changing housing patterns. The consistent popularity of retrievers suggests that traditional family-friendly breeds remain valued, even as new favorites emerge.

Regional devotion patterns reveal that dog ownership isn't just about having a pet—it's about making lifestyle choices that prioritize a dog's needs. This commitment varies significantly by state, suggesting that cultural factors, housing markets, and local attitudes all play roles in how Americans relate to their dogs.

As we look toward the future, these trends suggest that breed popularity will continue to evolve with changing lifestyles, housing patterns, and cultural influences. The French Bulldog's dominance may be challenged by new favorites, or it may represent a lasting shift toward smaller, more urban-adapted breeds. What remains constant is Americans' deep connection to their canine companions—a relationship that shapes not just breed choices, but life choices.
